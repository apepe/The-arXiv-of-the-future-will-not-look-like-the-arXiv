Discoverable, structured, semantic, machine-readable

One final but very important advantage that is tightly linked to having a repository of HTML, rather than PDF, papers is discoverability. The entire full text of papers - not just the title and abstract - will be indexed by search engines and scholarly repositories, boosting content visibility. Moreover, articles in HTML have a well-defined semantic structure, making them fully machine-readable objects. The arXiv of the future will rethink papers as APIs that access semantically structured content.

Conclusion

We have looked at the history of the arXiv, identifying a number of possible reasons that determined its success as the most popular online preprint repository. We argue that the arXiv flourished because it catered to technically savvy researchers with a long standing tradition of sharing and collaboration (physics, for example, is one of the most highly collaborative fields of research).  The simplicity of the site and its LaTeX-centric submission process, secured rapid growth within communities that were already used to taking full control of the typesetting process, and were comfortable with sharing their documents with colleagues.
We also argue that while the arXiv was quick to adopt technology early on, it has changed very little since it's launch, both in terms of operations as well as technology. This unwillingness or inability to foster new technology and We argue that should the arXiv continue to remain stagnant, it will be eclipsed by other services call to researchers to demand more. We believe that in the era of the web, sharing research via PDF will inevitably come to an end. Let us embrace new technologies and practices, just like the arXiv did nearly 30 years ago so that we might create a better way to share research.
